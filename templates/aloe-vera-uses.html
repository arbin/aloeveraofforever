Note: A Free Radical is simply a substance that has an uneven number of electrons.
The most important structural feature of an atom for determining its chemical behavior is the number of electrons it has orbiting around it.
Substances that have an even number of electrons tend not to react chemically.
However a free radical because of the uneven number of electrons it contains, will frantically attempt to gain the electron it needs to bring it back to even stability.
Generally, free radicals attack the nearest stable molecule, ’stealing’ its electron. When the ‘attacked’ molecule loses its electron it in turn becomes a free radical, beginning a chain reaction.
Once the process is started, it can have a domino effect, ultimately resulting in damage to important cellular components such as DNA or the cell membrane. Cells may function poorly or die if this occurs.
Free radical damage comes from exposure to pollution, toxins, sunlight, smoking, fried foods, exhaust fumes, stress as well as by natural biological and body processes.
Antioxidants on the other hand are molecules which can safely interact with free radicals and, by ‘donating’ one of their own electrons, terminate the chain reaction initiated by free radicals before vital molecules are damaged.
The human body has a defense system of antioxidants to prevent or minimize free radical damage but if antioxidants are unavailable, or if the production of free radicals exceeds the capability of available antioxidants, damage can occur. This danger increases with age.
